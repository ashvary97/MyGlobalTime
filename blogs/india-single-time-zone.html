Why India Has a Single Time Zone (And the Debate for More)

Published on August 16, 2025

India is a land of incredible diversity, from its languages and cultures to its vast and varied landscapes. But there's one area where the country is surprisingly uniform: its time. Despite stretching nearly 3,000 kilometers from east to west, the entire nation of over 1.4 billion people operates on a single time zone: India Standard Time (IST).

How is this possible? And is it actually a good idea? Let's explore the reasons behind India's single time zone and the ongoing debate to add another.

A Decision for Unity

The choice to have one time zone for the entire country was made after India gained independence in 1947. The primary goal was to foster national unity and simplicity. By establishing a single, standard time, the government aimed to reduce confusion and create a unified administrative system for railways, radio broadcasts, and government offices.

India Standard Time (UTC+5:30) was chosen as a compromise. Itâ€™s based on the longitude that runs through the city of Mirzapur in Uttar Pradesh, which is roughly in the center of the country. This decision meant that no part of the country would be drastically far from the standard meridian.

The Sun Doesn't Follow the Clock

While the single time zone works well on paper, it creates a significant mismatch with the natural rhythm of the sun, especially at the country's eastern and western extremities.

Consider this:

  • In Dong, Arunachal Pradesh, in the far northeast, the sun can rise as early as 4:15 AM.
  • In Ghuar Mota, Gujarat, in the far west, the sun rises almost two hours later, around 6:15 AM.

This discrepancy has real-world consequences. In the northeast, the sun is already high in the sky when offices and schools open at 10 AM, leading to a loss of productive daylight hours in the morning. By the time the workday ends, it's often dark. This mismatch can lead to lower productivity and higher electricity consumption for lighting.

The Debate for a Second Time Zone

For years, there has been a growing call, particularly from the northeastern states, to introduce a second time zone for India.

The Proposal: The most common suggestion is to create a new time zone, UTC+6:30, for the northeastern region. This would be one hour ahead of IST. Some have informally dubbed this proposed time zone "Chai Baganti Time" (Tea Garden Time), a nod to the region's famous tea plantations, where workers have long followed a schedule better aligned with the sun.

The Pros:

  • Energy Savings: More daylight during waking hours could save millions of units of electricity.
  • Increased Productivity: Aligning work schedules with natural daylight could boost the productivity and well-being of millions of people.
  • Better Quality of Life: People in the northeast would have more daylight in the evenings for leisure and family activities.

The Cons:

  • Logistical Chaos: A second time zone could create confusion for railway and flight schedules, which are currently synchronized across the country.
  • Potential for Accidents: The risk of accidents at the "border" of the two time zones could increase.
  • Threat to National Unity: Some fear that creating a separate time zone could foster a sense of division from the rest of the country.

A Timely Question

For now, India continues to operate on a single time zone. The government has researched the issue but has so far decided against making a change, citing the potential for chaos.

The debate, however, highlights a fascinating challenge: how does a country as vast and diverse as India balance the need for national unity with the practical, daily realities of its people? It's a question of time that is yet to be resolved.
